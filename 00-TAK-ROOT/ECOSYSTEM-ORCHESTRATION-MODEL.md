## SECTION 1 — THE FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE

### What the Ecosystem Is Designed To Do

The Theeb Ecosystem is a **networked system of specialized capabilities** that can be assembled — in any combination — to serve a client, execute a project, or build a product.

The power of the ecosystem is not in any single entity. It is in the **orchestration** — the ability to combine research, publishing, education, clinical delivery, financial infrastructure, and outcomes measurement into integrated solutions that no competitor can replicate.

### The Tension That Must Be Managed

```
INDEPENDENCE                     INTEROPERABILITY
──────────────────────           ──────────────────────────
Each entity has its own          Entities collaborate seamlessly
  legal identity                   across projects
  CR and licenses                Shared clients, shared data
  P&L and balance sheet            (with appropriate agreements)
  investor relationships          Shared strategic direction
  operational management          Shared quality standards
  brand and reputation            Revenue sharing frameworks

These are NOT contradictions. They are design features.
```

The ecosystem is designed so that **independence makes each entity more credible**, while **interoperability makes the whole greater than the sum of parts**.

## SECTION 2 — THE ORCHESTRATION FRAMEWORK

### How a Project Gets Assembled

```
STEP 1: INTAKE
Client or partner need identified
         |
STEP 2: MAPPING
Which ecosystem capabilities does this project require?
(Use the Capability Matrix — Section 4)
         |
STEP 3: LEAD ENTITY DESIGNATION
Which entity is closest to the client primary need?
That entity leads the relationship and coordinates others
         |
STEP 4: ACTIVATION
Each required entity is formally engaged
Inter-entity agreements signed (or referenced if standing)
Revenue allocation agreed before work begins
         |
STEP 5: DELIVERY
Each entity delivers its specific scope
Lead entity manages client interface
         |
STEP 6: REVENUE SETTLEMENT
Each entity invoices per agreed allocation
Client receives one integrated output
         |
STEP 7: KNOWLEDGE CAPTURE
Learnings documented and shared across ecosystem
Data assets (de-identified) added to shared intelligence layer
```

**What they need:** Saudi-specific clinical evidence, physician education, and market access support

|-------|--------|-------|-------------|

**Lead entity:** TKG — Nama CRO (research is the primary need)
**Total ecosystem revenue potential:** SAR 3–8M over 18 months (one drug, one indication)

### Example C: Scientific Society Strategic Partnership

**Client:** A mid-size Saudi scientific society (e.g., Saudi Endocrine Society)
**Problem:** Society is Category C, limited resources, no journal, minimal CME output

| Entity | Role | Revenue Model |
|--------|------|---------------|
| TKG — Waraqa | Launch peer-reviewed journal for the society; editorial infrastructure; indexing pathway | Revenue share on APC + pharma journal ads |
| TKG — Nama Academy | Deliver accredited CME using society faculty; co-organize annual conference | Revenue share on CME fees + pharma sponsorships |
| TKG — Evidence Platform | Society hub on the platform: member access, CME tracking, guidelines | SaaS subscription from society CME revenue |
| TKG — Nama CRO | Research collaboration; society experts as PIs; disease registry for the specialty | CRO services + grant facilitation |
| THDG — Welunion | Specialty clinics or CDM programs as research delivery sites | Clinical services data |
| TVG — VBH Company | Specialty outcomes data for the society research agenda | Analytics partnership |

**Revenue sharing with the society:**
- Society receives 40–60% of journal revenue (their name, their editorial authority)
- Society receives 40–50% of CME revenue (their faculty, their brand)
- Society retains 60% of research grant funds (their PIs)
- Ecosystem entities retain the balance for services provided

**Outcome for society:** Category A SCFHS rating (1,150+ points), SAR 1–2M annual revenue, national scientific credibility, zero operational burden

## SECTION 5 — INTER-ENTITY AGREEMENTS

### Standing Framework Agreement
A master inter-entity collaboration agreement exists at the ecosystem level, covering:
- Data sharing standards and PDPL compliance
- Revenue sharing principles
- IP ownership (each entity retains IP generated within its scope)
- Conflict resolution (internal first, then SCCA arbitration)
- Quality standards (aligned across all entities)

### Project-Specific Addenda
Each project adds a short addendum specifying:
- Scope of each entity
- Revenue allocation percentages
- Deliverables and timeline
- Client-facing responsibilities
- Confidentiality obligations

### IP Ownership Rule

| IP Type | Owner |
|---------|-------|
| Clinical data generated at THDG sites | THDG entity (or patient, per PDPL) |
| Research protocols and methods | TKG — Nama CRO |
| Published guidelines and journals | TKG — Waraqa (with society co-ownership) |
| Platform code and architecture | TKG — Evidence Platform |
| Analytics models and algorithms | TVG entity that built them |
| Brand and trademarks | Each entity owns its own brand |
| Ecosystem-level data benchmarks | Theeb Ecosystem (shared asset) |

## SECTION 6 — PRINCIPLES FOR EVERY ENTITY

Every entity in the ecosystem must:

1. **Know what it does best** — deep specialty, not generalist
2. **Know what others do** — understand the full capability matrix
3. **Actively identify collaboration opportunities** — flag projects that could activate multiple entities
4. **Never turn away a client** — if the need is outside your scope, route it to the right entity and stay involved in your piece
5. **Document inter-entity work** — maintain records of cross-entity projects for revenue settlement and learning
6. **Honor the quality standard** — your work reflects on every other entity in the ecosystem

### The Ecosystem is Only as Strong as Its Weakest Collaboration

A single poor delivery by one entity in a collaborative project damages the reputation of every entity involved. Quality and professionalism are ecosystem-wide standards, not entity-specific choices.

## SECTION 7 — ORCHESTRATION GOVERNANCE

### Decision Authority

| Scenario | Decision Maker |
|----------|----------------|
| Client approaches one entity with multi-entity need | That entity GM identifies opportunity, routes to ecosystem coordination |
| New project requires cross-group coordination | Group leads meet to determine scope and lead entity |
| Revenue share dispute | Resolved per inter-entity agreement; escalated to founder if needed |
| Strategic partnership (pharma MNC, government) | Founder-level decision on entity activation and structure |

### The Core Commercial Principle

No entity should pursue a project that clearly requires another entity's capabilities without bringing them in. The integrated offering always commands a higher value than the sum of individual scopes.

A pharma company can hire any CRO. They cannot hire another ecosystem.
